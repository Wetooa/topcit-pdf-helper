# Intellectual Property Rights & Business Ethics: A Learning Guide

## 1. Copyright Registration Essentials

To register a copyright (or program copyright for software), you generally need:
*   **Application Statement:** Official form for copyright registration.
*   **Copies of the Work:**
    *   For general works: Drawings, photos, or electronic media.
    *   For computer programs: An electronic copy of the program.
    *   *Note:* A partial excerpt of program content may be submitted if it proves creation. This excerpt should be in program language before computer conversion.
*   **Proof of Registration Reason:** (Only if required)
*   **List of Stakeholders:** If there are multiple authors, heirs, rights holders, etc., provide a list.
*   **List of Works:** For large-quantity registrations (multiple works, neighboring works, databases).

**Outcome:** The Korea Copyright Commission issues a **Copyright Registration Certificate** upon successful application.

                                                    *   **Patent:** Right to an invention (e.g., manufacturing method). Duration: 20 years from application date. Not applicable if publicly known/used before filing.
*   **Trademark:** Right to a mark identifying one's goods/services.
    *   **Mark:** Symbols, letters, figures, sounds, smells, holograms, movements, or colors. Trade names can also be registered.
    *   **Service Mark:** Trademark for services (e.g., academies). Website domains can be registered as service marks (a registered service mark takes precedence over a registered domain).
    *   Duration: Can be used virtually permanently by renewing every 10 years.
*   **Design:** Right to the aesthetic form, shape, color, or composition of an article or font. Duration: 20 years from registration date.
*   **Utility Model:** Right to protect practical designs, typically for improving the shape and structure of existing products to enhance practicality. Lower technical level than patents. Duration: 10 years from application date.

**Acquisition of IPR:**
*   Acquired by **registering with the Korean Intellectual Property Office**.
*   **First-to-file rule** applies.
*   Protection is **country-specific**: rights are recognized only in the country where registration is made; separate applications are needed in each country for international protection.
*   **Purpose:** Grant exclusive rights for a certain period to the creator, promoting technological and industrial development, after which anyone can use it.

**Violations of Industrial Property Rights:**
These involve misleading the public about the registration status of products or services.

*   **False Patent Claims:**
    *   Displaying an unregistered/non-existent patent number.
    *   Marking a product as patented when the application isn't complete.
    *   Using marks easily confused with patent marks.
    *   Marking a utility model, design, or trademark as a patent.
*   **False Trademark Claims:**
    *   Displaying an unregistered mark as if it were a registered or applied-for trademark.
    *   Using false trademarks in commercial materials (ads, signboards, packaging).
    *   Theft of renowned domestic and foreign trademarks.
*   **False Utility Model/Design Claims:**
    *   Marking an item as a utility model/design registration/application when it's not registered or pending.
    *   Displaying marks likely to be confused with registered utility models or designs.

**Punishment for False Display of IPR:**
*   Imprisonment for up to 3 years or a fine of up to 20 million won.
*   This is an **offense not subject to complaint**, meaning a third party (not just the victim) can file criminal charges.

## 3. New Intellectual Property Rights

**Definition:** These rights protect emerging areas like computer programs, biotechnology, semiconductor designs, and digital content, which are difficult to protect with traditional IPRs (like patents and copyrights).

**Types of New Intellectual Property Rights:**

*   **Industrial Copyright:** Computer programs, artificial intelligence, databases.
*   **High-Tech Industrial Property Rights:** Semiconductor integrated circuit layout design, biotechnology.
*   **Information Property Rights:** Trade secrets, multimedia.

**WTO Scope:** The World Trade Organization (WTO) broadly includes these new rights within its definition of intellectual property, covering all rights from intellectual activities in industry, science, literature, or art.

## 4. Unfair Competition Prevention & Trade Secret Protection

### A) Unfair Competition Prevention

**Concept:** Aims to prevent unfair business practices, such as misusing well-known trademarks/trade names and infringing on trade secrets.

**Acts of Unfair Competition include:**

*   **Misusing Well-Known Marks:** Using identical or similar names, trade names, trademarks, or product packaging of another widely known person/company in Korea.
*   **False Origin/Quality Marks:** Making false claims about the origin, quality, content, manufacturing process, usage, or quantity of goods in advertising or on products.
*   **Agent/Representative Misuse of Trademarks:** Using a trademark registered in another country (by Paris Convention/WTO members) without good cause by an agent or former agent.
*   **Domain Name Squatting:** Registering, holding, transferring, or using a domain name identical or similar to another's well-known name/trademark without legitimate right, for purposes like selling it, preventing legitimate use, or gaining commercial profit.
*   **Copying Product Shapes:** Transferring, lending, exhibiting, importing, or exporting goods whose shape has been copied from another's manufactured goods.
*   **Misusing Business Ideas/Information:** Unfairly using another person's technical or business ideas (with economic value) obtained during negotiations (e.g., business proposals, auctions) for one's own or a third party's business interests, or providing such information to others to exploit.

### B) Trade Secret Protection

**Concept of a Trade Secret:** Information (like production methods, sales methods, technical/business information) that is:
1.  **Not publicly known:** The information is a secret.
2.  **Has independent economic value:** It's useful for business activities and has financial worth.
3.  **Managed as a secret:** Efforts have been made to keep it confidential (e.g., confidentiality notices, access restrictions, confidentiality agreements). *Information accessible to anyone is not a trade secret.*

**Acts Constituting Infringement of Trade Secrets:**

*   **Improper Acquisition & Use/Disclosure:** Acquiring trade secrets through theft, deception, coercion, or other improper means, then using or disclosing them.
*   **Knowledgeable Use/Disclosure:** Using or disclosing trade secrets after acquiring them, knowing they were improperly obtained, or without such knowledge due to gross negligence.
*   **Breach of Confidentiality:** Using or disclosing trade secrets to gain improper benefits or harm the owner while under a contractual or other duty to maintain secrecy.

**Precedents for Infringement of Trade Secrets:**
*   Adding only new features based on another's source code.
*   Developing and selling substantially similar programs by performing **reverse analysis** (reverse engineering) of program code, especially if it violates a contract or technical protection measures.

**Precedents that are NOT Infringement of Trade Secrets:**
*   When the programming language and implementation method of software are completely different.
*   Performing reverse analysis of legally purchased program code to obtain information necessary for compatibility, *when such information cannot be easily obtained otherwise* (Article 101-4 of Copyright Act).
